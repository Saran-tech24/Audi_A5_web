Audi AG

Audi AG is a German luxury automotive manufacturer headquartered in Ingolstadt, Bavaria. A subsidiary of the Volkswagen Group, Audi produces premium vehicles globally and has established itself as a leader in innovation and performance.

How Did Audi Get Its Name?

The company's founder, August Horch, established his first automobile manufacturing company in 1899, called "August Horch & Cie." He left this company in 1909 due to conflicts with his business partners. After leaving, he founded a new company in Zwickau on 16 July 1909, originally named August Horch Automobilwerke GmbH. However, his former partners filed a lawsuit against him for trademark infringement, preventing him from using the "Horch" brand.

During a meeting with business friends, Franz Fikentscher’s son, studying Latin at the time, suggested "Audi" as an alternative to "Horch." Since "Horch" means "listen" in German, "Audi" carried the same meaning in Latin. The idea was enthusiastically accepted, and on 25 April 1910, the company was officially registered as Audi Automobilwerke GmbH Zwickau.

The Meaning of Four Rings in Audi

Audi's iconic four-ring logo represents a significant period in the company’s history. On 29 June 1932, Audiwerke, Horchwerke, and Zschopauer Motorenwerke J. S. Rasmussen AG (DKW) merged to form Auto Union AG, initiated by the State Bank of Saxony. At the same time, a purchase agreement was made with Wanderer to acquire its motor vehicle division.

To symbolize the unity and collaboration of the four companies, a new logo was created—four interlinked rings, each representing one of the merging manufacturers. This logo signified strength and solidarity, making Auto Union AG the second-largest motor vehicle manufacturer in Germany.

Within Auto Union AG, brands had distinct roles: DKW focused on motorcycles and small cars, Wanderer produced midsize cars, Audi operated in the deluxe midsize segment, and Horch manufactured high-end luxury cars. Today, Audi continues to represent innovation, performance, and a legacy defined by its four rings.
